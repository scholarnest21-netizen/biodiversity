What is Biodiversity?

Section 4.1

Types of Biodiversity

Biodiversity can be described at three hierarchical levels, each measuring a different aspect of life's variety.

01

Genetic Diversity

Each species stores an immense amount of genetic information. Variations among genes of the same species are called genetic diversity.

🌾 Example: India was home to 400,000 rice varieties during the Vedic period. Even today, 200,000 exist!
02

Species Diversity

Diversity arising due to variations among species present in specific ecosystems. Each species plays a unique role.

🦁 Example: Horses & donkeys are distinct species; lions & tigers are distinct species.
03

Ecosystem Diversity

Refers to the diversity of habitats including the different life forms within them, and the variety of ecosystems within a biogeographical or political boundary.

🌊 Example: Forests, grasslands, wetlands, coral reefs — each is a distinct ecosystem.

Section 4.2

Value of Biodiversity

Biodiversity is essential to human survival in multiple dimensions — from basic survival to aesthetic pleasure.

🍽️

Survival Value

Human beings would perish without biodiversity. Most communities, like those in Ghana, depend entirely on local biodiversity for food, medicine, and livelihood from animals such as antelopes, snails, and rabbits.

🏥

Health & Healing

A large percentage of the world's population uses medicines derived from plants. Traditional knowledge is increasingly important. India should include medicinal plants under the Indian Medicine Central Council Act, 1970.

🌾

Food Security

All food requirements are met by the biological world. Cereals, pulses, vegetables, fruits, milk, honey — all are outcomes of biodiversity. India is home to 167 important plant species including spices like asafoetida, ginger, turmeric, and mint.

🔗

Ecological Services

Species evolve to fill niches. A single tree provides habitat, conserves soil and water, and keeps air clean. The value of ecosystem services is estimated between $16–54 trillion per year (Nature, 1997).

💰

Productive Value

Products used by humans: cinchona plant for medicine, wood as fuel, charcoal, dyes. Every macaw in a Costa Rica bird reserve earns an average of USD 15,000/year. Millions visit the Himalayas for the Valley of Flowers.

⚖️

Ethical Value

Each species has the right to exist. Ethical use has deep roots in human culture, religion, and society. Biodiversity can be retained only if humans put in sincere efforts — it is extremely important to realize how biodiversity can be utilized.

🎨

Aesthetic Pleasure

Each species and ecosystem adds richness and beauty to life on Earth. Watching a sun set over an ocean, the sight of a leaping deer, the sound of a singing bird — all add to Earth's aesthetic wealth. Ecotourism, bird watching, wildlife photography, gardening all depend on biodiversity.

🌍

Value for Developing Nations

Recognizing sovereign rights over genetic resources benefits nations like India. India holds most of the world's genetic material and an immensely rich indigenous knowledge base. The first IPR benefit-sharing case in India: the Kani tribe and the anti-fatigue drug Jeevani.

📚 Slow Loss of Biodiversity

Monocultures and the Loss of Varieties

Just 14 mammal and bird species account for 90% of our food supply from animals. Just four species — wheat, maize, rice, and potato — provide half our energy from plants. This focus on monocultures may result in extinction of species, varieties, and breeds.

Section 4.3

Hotspots of Biodiversity

Biodiversity is not evenly distributed. Areas that are biologically rich but under great danger are called biodiversity hotspots.

What are Biodiversity Hotspots?

Hotspots are the richest and most threatened reservoirs of plant and animal life on Earth. They are areas of exceptionally rich concentration of species with high endemism and are extremely vulnerable to anthropogenic disturbances.

25 hotspots have been identified worldwide, covering about 1.4% of Earth's land area.

🇮🇳 Western Ghats
🇮🇳 North-East Himalaya

The concept was developed by Norman Meyers in 1988 to designate priority areas for in situ conservation.

3 Criteria to Identify a Hotspot
i

The number of species present

ii

The number of species that exist exclusively in the given ecosystem (endemic species)

iii

The degree of threat they face from human activities

Location of India's Biodiversity Hotspots

Most hotspots are located in tropical areas in developing countries. About 85% of the rich ecosystems for food, fuel wood, and other forest products depend on them.

The Western Ghats and North-East Himalaya are rich in endemic species of reptiles, amphibians, insects, and mammals.

What are Endemic Species?

Plants or animals restricted to a limited geographic area are called endemic species. They are confined to a particular region due to geographical barriers like sea, valley, or mountains. They adjust poorly to the environment in wide areas and possess low ecological amplitude.
Examples: Elettaria repens, Ficus religiosa, Butea monosperma, Ficus bengalensis
⚠️ Restricted habitat makes endemic species extremely vulnerable to anthropogenic disturbances and introduction of new species.
📌 EDGE Species

Evolutionarily Distinct and Globally Endangered

EDGE species are unique in looks, lifestyle, and behaviour and are on the verge of extinction. Once extinct, there will be no species like them — they have very few close relatives. Examples: elephants, pandas (well known), Yangtze River dolphin (world's rarest cetacean), bumblebee bat (smallest mammal on Earth), and echidnas. Alarmingly, little is being done for conservation of about two-thirds of the top 100 EDGE mammal species.

Section 4.5

Threats to Biodiversity

Human interference has placed biodiversity under constant threat. Continuous and large-scale loss is causing severe imbalances in the biosphere.

Major Anthropogenic Threats to Biodiversity
🏗️

Habitat Destruction

Cutting trees, filling wetlands, burning forests, building dams and industrial plants destroy the natural habitat. Large dams submerge tracts of forest and introduce human settlements into forest areas.

🔫

Poaching & Over-Exploitation

Tigers, elephants killed for skin, tusks, claws. Snakes and birds caught as collectors' items. The pharmaceutical industry exploits many plants. Ranthambore poachers use local Mogya tribes as trackers.

🏭

Environmental Pollution

Soil, water, and air pollution disturbs ecosystem functioning. Overuse of pesticides declines fish-eating birds and falcons. Lead poisoning kills ducks, swans, cranes. Industrial effluents destroy coral reefs.

🌡️

Global Climate Change

Climate change will adversely affect biodiversity. Species that cannot adapt to a warmer climate will become extinct. Islands and coastal systems face particularly high biodiversity losses due to rising sea levels.

🌿

Invasion by Introduced Species

Foreign (alien or exotic) species pose a great threat to endemic species. Lantana camara from Brazil is spreading rapidly in Indian forests. Water hyacinth is clogging rivers and lakes.

⚖️

Weak Legal Systems

Protection of wildlife in India is disreputably negligent. National parks lack rangers. Villagers are allowed to live within sanctuaries, leading to conflicts. The Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 curtails community rights.

⛏️

Mining Activities

Mining involves digging up tons of earth, leading to deforestation and soil erosion. It also leads to water pollution with dangerous chemicals. Mining in Venezuelan Amazon severely threatens rich biodiversity.

🌊

Exploitation of Water Resources

Over-exploitation of surface water creates scarcity. In the absence of rainfall and water pollution, aquatic diversity and survival of aquatic species is threatened.

🔥

Forest Fires

Common causes: lightning, human carelessness, volcanic eruption. Forest fires due to natural or man-made reasons seriously harm biodiversity. In 2003, fires burned 300,000 ha in Portugal, threatening cork and oak forests.

Section 4.6

Conservation of Biodiversity

We are losing about 1,500 species every two months. There are two major approaches — in situ (on site) and ex situ (off site) conservation.

In Situ Conservation

Conservation in natural habitat

Plants and animals are conserved in their natural habitats. Applies only to wild flora and fauna, not domesticated species.

  • National Parks (90 in India)
  • Wildlife Sanctuaries (492 in India)
  • Biosphere Reserves (13 in India)
Advantages: Long-term protection · Natural ecosystems in large areas allow evolution · Cheaper means of protection

Ex Situ Conservation

Conservation outside natural habitat

Species are conserved outside their natural habitats through scientific management.

  • Gene Banks (Germplasm Banks)
  • Botanical Gardens
  • Zoos & Aquaria
  • Tissue Culture Technique
  • DNA Technology
Advantages: Long-term species conservation · Controlled supervision · Quality of offspring improved by genetic techniques

Wildlife Sanctuary

Dedicated to protecting wildlife species only. Boundary is determined by state legislation. Hunting without permit is prohibited. Grazing and cattle movement are regulated. Comprises core, buffer, and restoration zones.
🇮🇳 492 Wildlife Sanctuaries in India

Biosphere Reserve

Conservation of natural resources and improvement of relationship between humans and environment. Contains both natural and human-influenced ecosystems with substantial human settlement.
🇮🇳 13 Biosphere Reserves in India

Ex Situ Methods in Detail

🏦 Gene Banks (Germplasm Banks)

Seeds, pollen grains, and vegetative parts preserved under viable (frozen) conditions. Seeds of maize and barley can survive thousands of years. Millets, oil seeds, vegetables, pulses, and narcotics can be stored in liquid nitrogen.

🌿 Botanical Gardens

Used for conservation of rare and endangered plant species, for study and research of specific plant characters, and for disseminating scientific information to promote sustainable development.

🔬 Tissue Culture Technique

Also called micro-propagation. A very small piece of shoot apex, leaf section, or even an individual cell is cut and placed in a sterile culture containing special culture medium. Used for rapid multiplication of plants — especially useful for sugarcane and others with very few seeds.

🧬 DNA Technology

Enables whole DNA of a plant or animal cell to be conserved. Through recombinant DNA, genes important for conservation can be isolated and used in other species. Provides an innovative approach for biodiversity conservation.

🏛️ Central Acts for Conservation

Key Legislation in India

Major Acts: Forest Act 1927 · Wildlife (Protection) Act 1972 · Forest (Conservation) Act 1980 · Environment (Protection) Act 1986. Key policies: National Forest Policy (1988) · National Conservation Strategy · National Agricultural Policy · National Fisheries Policy · National Policy and Action Strategy on Biodiversity · National Wildlife Action Plan.

Phytogeographical Regions of India

🗺️ Geographical Diversity

India is a vast geographic region. It comprises the Himalayan region, Ganga plains, Thar Desert, and Deccan plateau. India represents almost all kinds of geographic variations found elsewhere in the world.

🌤️ Climatic Diversity

Climatic conditions range from cold temperate and frost-covered Himalayan regions to low rainfall dry tropics in the north-west. Divided into four climatic regions: wet zone (>200cm), intermediate (100–200cm), dry (50–100cm), and arid zone (<50cm).

🦋 Biological Diversity

Divided into nine phytogeographical regions. One of the 12 primary centres of origin of cultivated plants and domesticated animals. Home to 167 important plant species and 114 breeds of domesticated animals.

🏔️ Habitat Diversity

India represents almost all types of habitats of the world — high mountain ranges, hills, plateau, plains, deserts, etc. Freshwater and saline lakes, reservoirs, ponds, rivers, and streams provide diversity of habitats for aquatic plants and animals.

Section 4.7

Biogeographical Classification of India

India has been divided into nine phytogeographical regions based on geographical features.

(i) Western Himalayas Extends from central Kumaon to north-west Kashmir. Further classified into temperate, subtropical, and alpine regions. Vegetation: fir, birch, deodar, mango, sheesham, pine.
(ii) Eastern Himalayas Regions of Sikkim extending to highlands of Assam. Warmer with higher rainfall. Known as the wetland of Himalayas. Vegetation: fir, pine, sal, deodar, oak, birch.
(iii) Western Deserts Covers Haryana, Punjab, Rajasthan, and Gujarat. Annual rainfall <70 cm. High temperature. Xerophytic plants dominate: babool, kikar, khejri, amla, rohida.
(iv) Gangetic Plains Includes Ganga valley, parts of UP, Bihar, and Bengal. Highly fertile alluvial soil provides favourable conditions. Key trees: mango, teak, sal, mulberry, figs.
(v) Central India Parts of Madhya Pradesh, Orissa, and Northern Gujarat between plains of Ganges and Southern plateau. Heavy rainfall. Dominant species: sal, sagwaan, bidi, mahua, mango.
(vi) Western Coast (Malabar Coast) Southern Gujarat to Kanyakumari. Very high rainfall, thick and dense trees: mahogany, cinchona, rubber, coconut, banana.
(vii) Deccan Plateau Entire peninsular India except Western Ghats. Includes parts of Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, and Karnataka. Dry and rocky terrain.
(viii) North-East India Comprises valleys of Brahmaputra. Receives the heaviest rainfall. Dense evergreen forests are common in this area.
(ix) Andaman & Nicobar Islands Several small and large islands near the equator. Wide range of forests. Vegetation ranges from coastal vegetation to evergreen forests.

Section 4.9

Biodiversity at Global Level

At the global level, there are about 82 million known species of living organisms. The expected total is about 50 million. More diversity occurs in tropics than elsewhere.

❄️ Arctic Zone
Near the poles. Algae, annual flowering plants, mosses, lichens. Ground vegetation: pteridophytes, orchids, insectivorous plants.
🌲 Northern Temperate Zone
Parts of USA and Canada. Gymnospermous tall trees, oaks, maples, chestnuts. At high altitude, grassy vegetation with herbaceous plants.
🌿 Southern Temperate Zone
Southern Africa, Australia, New Zealand. Ferns and gymnosperms. New Zealand is richest in bryophytes. Palms, nuts, Acacia and Casurina in Australia.
🌺 Tropical Zone
Tropical Africa, Asia, Mexico, South America. Xerophytes in dry areas. On mountain peaks, grasses. In Mexico: orange, banana, mango are common trees.

Five Geographical Regions (Based on Fauna)

🐯 Oriental Region Tiger, neelgai, rabbit, flying fox, gharial, peacocks. Characterized by plain forests, elevated areas. Rainfall sufficient in most parts.
🦘 Australian Region Deserts and dry plains. Kangaroo, parrots, scale-footed lizards, honey eaters, crowned pigeons. Freshwater ecosystems are rare.
🦅 Neotropical Region South America, Central America, West Indies. American monkeys, dogs, deer, cattle, squirrels, electric eel, monkey birds, humming birds.
🦆 Nearctic Region Greenland and North America except Mexico. Coniferous forests, tundra, islands, lakes, seas. Flying squirrels, vultures, kingfishers, ducks, flamingo, alligators, rattle snake, cranes.
🦎 Palearctic Region Largest geographical region. Includes Europe, northern Africa, and Asia. Fauna represented by 135 families of terrestrial vertebrates.

Biodiversity at National & Local Level

🇮🇳 National Level

India has a rich biodiversity owing to wide variety of physical and climatic factors. Biodiversity is peculiar and highly categorized. Most is localized to Western Ghats, north-western Himalayas, and Andaman & Nicobar Islands. India is a 'Vavilov Centre' — an original centre for domestication of crop plants.

📍 Local Level

The variety of species found at a given place constitutes the biodiversity at local level. The reason for local biodiversity is the local changes in climatic, geomorphic, and ecological factors. In Rajasthan: Desert (low rainfall, high temperature, sand dunes) · Mountain/Hilly region (Aravalli, tiger, panther) · Plains (eastern Aravalli).

📋 Chapter Summary

Everything present around us, whether visible or invisible, is an integral part of the vast biodiversity of the biosphere. It is found at three levels: genetic, species, and ecosystem diversity.
Biodiversity is the basis of human survival. Man is dependent on plants and animals for food, medicine, clothing, and many other aspects.
Scientists have identified 25 biodiversity hotspots worldwide — biologically rich areas under greatest threat. Two are found in India: Western Ghats and North-East Himalaya.
Plant or animal species on the verge of extinction are called endangered species. The Red Data Book lists about 25,000 endangered species all over the world.
Species confined to a particular region are called endemic species. India is home to a number of endemic species — about 4,900 flowering plants and 62% of known amphibians.
Major threats to biodiversity: deforestation, poaching, urbanization, environmental pollution, mining, climate change, invasive species, and weak legal systems.
Two major conservation approaches: in situ (national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves) and ex situ (gene banks, zoos, botanical gardens, tissue culture, DNA technology).
India is considered a biodiversity-rich nation and a megadiversity country — one of only 17 in the world. It has been divided into nine phytogeographical regions based on geographical features.
There are 17 megadiversity countries all over the world. India is one of them — full of biodiversity having all natural resources for all kinds of living organisms, and also extremely rich in cultural, geographical, and climatic diversity.
